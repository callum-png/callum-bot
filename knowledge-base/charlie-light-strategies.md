## QRT Strategy (The Standalone Test)

**The #1 rule:** The reader must understand your QRT within 10 seconds WITHOUT reading or watching the quoted post.

- If someone sees ONLY your QRT without the quoted tweet, it should STILL make sense and be engaging
- Worst-performing QRTs: ones that reference something 30 seconds into the quoted video — nobody watches that far before looking at your QRT
- "Can't believe this is free" type QRTs = low viral potential. No reason for non-followers to engage. You might get some of the influencer's followers to check it out, but there's no reason for thousands of new people to interact.
- For complex/boring launches: make the QRT a standalone joke or story. Don't rely on the mother post.
- The QRT should ideally work as its own post that could go viral even WITHOUT the quote underneath it

**What to do instead:**
- Write a meme or joke that stands on its own. The quote just happens to be the context.
- Use the influencer's proven formats (search their top posts) and rework them to reference the topic
- Find proven viral hooks from OTHER posts and apply them to this QRT

## Viral Format Reworking System

**Never write from scratch when you can rework a proven format.**

1. Save every viral post you see (organized by month in bookmarks)
2. When writing: browse saved formats, find one that worked, rework it for your topic
3. Copy proven opening lines/hooks — they're already validated
4. Go BACK 3+ months for formats people have forgotten. Don't reuse something from 5 days ago — that's stale and people will recognize it.
5. Take another creator's viral post → ask: "How can I use the same wording/format but make a different point or add a twist?"

**For a specific influencer:**
- Pull up ALL their top posts (search for posts with 1000+ likes)
- Find the common themes — most creators have ~10 categories of jokes they rotate
- Also pull up OTHER viral posts from the timeline
- Combine: the influencer's proven format + a trending topic/hook = maximum viral potential

**Charlie's process:**
- Scrolls his bookmark folder until he finds a format that fits the current topic
- Takes the OPENING LINE from a viral post and reworks it for the new context
- When something dramatic happens (news, launch, controversy), collects the dramatic opening lines people are using and saves them for later

## Storytelling Format (Charlie's Bread & Butter)

**Structure:**
- Each line = ONE short sentence. No blocks of text.
- First line = hook with specific dollar amount or dramatic claim
- Build with escalating details (each sentence creates a question only the next answers)
- Twist at the end that subverts expectations
- First-person stories ALWAYS outperform third-person descriptions

**Hook formulas that work:**
- "Met a guy who makes $X doing Y"
- "My friend made $10M last year as a 23-year-old. I asked how he did it."
- "I'm in shock" + absurd reveal
- "Years ago, I was [dramatic setup]..."
- "Hired 50 people. Told them to [absurd instruction]."
- "[Dramatic personal event]. [One sentence of context]."

**Categories Charlie rotates (~10 proven categories):**
1. Profiting off family members (son's lemonade stand → vending machine hustle)
2. Parody feel-good LinkedIn stories (helped someone → twist: made huge profit)
3. "My friend makes $X" → reveal it's something absurd
4. Fake business ideas played completely straight
5. QRTs of serious news with deadpan "What does this mean for me?"
6. Reworking other viral formats with the "chase" spin (making money off everything)
7. Taking the OPPOSITE point of a viral post using the same format
8. Airport/travel hustle schemes
9. Job interview power plays
10. "Everyone stood up and applauded" (satirical closers)

**Key insight:** Once you recognize a format works, you can reuse it forever by changing the topic. The format is the vehicle, the topic is the cargo.

## Intentional Errors (Engagement Hack)

**Hierarchy of engagement errors:**
1. **Factual errors** (BEST) — "The CEO of Microsoft, Bill Gates" → dozens of "he's not CEO anymore" replies, each one boosting engagement
2. **Logical impossibilities** — "sitting in first class in the middle seat" → "first class doesn't have middle seats, you moron" = engagement
3. **Typos in key words** — misspelling "entrepreneur" while flexing money = irony people can't resist pointing out
4. **Minor spelling errors** — natural typos you don't fix

**@ThreeYearLetterman approach:** 2-3 factual errors per post. Each error generates its own comment thread. People who didn't even read the whole post will stop to correct one error.

**Rules:**
- If you notice a typo after posting: DON'T FIX IT. Comments pointing it out = more engagement.
- The more "wrinkles" (debatable points) in your post, the more jumping-off points for engagement
- Even readers who don't finish the story might comment on one error they spotted
- Factual errors > spelling errors because corrections feel more urgent to people

## Batch Writing Strategy

**When writing for multiple influencers, group by category:**
- All finance/hustle meme accounts → write them together
- All tech/AI accounts → write them together
- All sports accounts → write them together

**Why:** Ideas flow more freely once you're in the rhythm of a category. First 2-3 drafts are hardest. After that, you'll get 5-10 good ideas at once. Don't jump between unrelated account types.

## Influencer Selection Criteria

**Don't look at follower count. Look at baseline likes.**

- 200K followers + 50 likes per post = BAD (audience not paying attention, account pivoted or built following long ago)
- 50K followers + consistent 500 likes per post = GOOD (engaged, active audience)
- Check for CONSISTENCY — not just occasional viral spikes
- The ratio of average likes to followers is the key metric
- Occasional viral posts with 5000 likes but regular posts at 50 likes = audience isn't really there

## Paid Disclaimers (Campaign Guidance)

- For meme QRTs: gray area. Not necessarily required if it's commentary/reaction, not a direct endorsement.
- Don't mention paid disclaimers to influencers — let them decide their own approach.
- If the post is a meme or commentary that can't be directly tied to a paid partnership, don't bring it up.
- The creator is ultimately the one posting — it's their interpretation of the rules.
- Avoid being the party that advises on legal risk.
